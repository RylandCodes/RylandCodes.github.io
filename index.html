Introduction to Semakau Landfill

Semakau Landfill is Singapore’s only landfill, located on an island south of the mainland. Despite being a landfill, the site has been designed to be environmentally friendly, helping to preserve the surrounding marine life. However, with the growing volume of waste, environmental issues related to the landfill are becoming increasingly pressing.

Semakau Landfill

Why is Semakau Filling Up?

Singaporeans produce around 2,000 tonnes of waste daily, most of which ends up in Semakau Landfill. Despite this, only about 12% of households actually recycle, which is the lowest rate in a decade. Even among recyclables, much of the waste remains unrecyclable due to contamination. Many Singaporeans mistakenly dispose of non-recyclable items or fail to clean their recyclables, rendering entire batches unusable. Living in a clean environment, many do not see recycling as a priority. For example, some discard plastic items without rinsing them or leave food inside containers, contaminating the materials and reducing their chances of being successfully recycled.

Increased consumption in Singapore leads to more waste being generated. In contrast, Sweden is an example of a highly efficient recycler, with a domestic recycling rate of over 99%, compared to Singapore’s mere 12%.

What Happens If Semakau Fills Up

If Semakau Landfill reaches capacity, it will pose a challenge for land-scarce Singapore. Expanding landfills would reduce space for housing, infrastructure, and green areas, worsening the country’s space constraints.

Additionally, a landfill on mainland Singapore would cause environmental issues, including pollution from methane gas and leachate, which could contaminate groundwater. This would harm local ecosystems and contribute to climate change.

Pollution also poses health risks, as poor air quality and contaminated water sources can lead to respiratory issues and other diseases.

Overall, a landfill on mainland Singapore would harm the environment, reduce residents’ quality of life, and hinder the country’s progress in waste management.

Solutions YOU can do to Prevent a Waste Crisis

There are small steps we can take to address this issue. In Sweden, over 99% of household waste is recycled or reused. The country’s system holds producers responsible for recycling or disposing of their products, including packaging, electronics, and batteries. This approach has led to a high recycling rate. By following Sweden’s example, we can improve recycling rates and extend Semakau Landfill’s lifespan.

Some cool ideas you can implement

Use reusable bags, bottles, and containers

Using reusable alternatives to single-use plastics is one of the simplest and most effective ways to reduce waste. Plastic bags, bottles, and containers are often used only once before being discarded, contributing to pollution and landfill waste. By switching to reusable versions, you can drastically reduce the number of plastic items you use.

Go paperless

Opting for paperless options is a great way to reduce waste and save trees. From digital banking to online documents and notes, moving away from paper-based systems reduces the demand for paper production, which in turn reduces deforestation and energy consumption.

Choose sustainable packaging

Sustainable packaging options help reduce the environmental impact of products. By choosing items that use minimal or recyclable packaging, you can make a difference in reducing waste, as packaging often makes up a large percentage of the waste stream.

Looking Forward

Singapore aims to reduce daily waste sent to Semakau Landfill by 30% by 2030. Singaporeans can help delay its filling by recycling and reducing unnecessary purchases. By making these changes, we can extend Semakau Landfill’s lifespan. We can do this!
